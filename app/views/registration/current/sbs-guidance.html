Moving to .gov.uk domain: Step by step

If you're moving to .gov.uk from another top-level domain, like .org.uk or .co.uk, here are some best practices to help you plan that transition.

  1. Step 1: Start the conversation with your technical and communications staff

    Identify who you’ll work with from your IT team (e.g., folks responsible for DNS, web, network, information security), public affairs (people responsible for public communication online, in print, or elsewhere), and administrative staff. Think about the internal approvals you’ll need for a new .gov.uk domain.

    1. Check if your organisation can get a .gov.uk domain name
    2. Get permission to apply for a .gov.uk domain name

  2. Step 2: Choose your .gov.uk domain name

    Come up with .gov.uk domain options that meet our naming requirements.

    We’ll try to give you your preferred domain. We’ll make sure your request meets our requirements. We’ll work with you to find the best domain for your organisation.

    When you choose your .gov.uk domain name you must make sure it is:

    • available - you can check if a domain name is registered
    • descriptive
    • unique
    • not confusing for users

    Use this guidance to make sure that you choose an appropriate .gov.uk domain name.

  3. Step 3: Get ready to register

    Get ready to complete the .gov.uk domain registration.

    Make sure you have all the information you need to complete the .gov.uk domain registration. We'll ask you about your organization and the .gov.uk domain you want. You'll need to have a GOV.UK Domains account to request a .gov.uk domain.

    1. Identify a .gov.uk domain name administrator
    2. Create an account Identity checks and link to organization

  5. Step 4: Find DNS hosting services

    We don't provide DNS (Domain Name System) hosting services. You'll need to operate or manage authoritative DNS for your domain. If you're coming from a non-.gov domain, this may be a new requirement. You can manage your own DNS servers or host your domain with a third party. "DNS hosting" is a good search term to find providers. Options range in price but are typically only a few dollars per month. Some local internet service providers (ISPs) or technology service providers may offer DNS hosting.

  6. Step 5: Audit your existing web content

    Consider using this transition as an opportunity to audit your web content. Archive content that is no longer relevant or update it to make it current.

    Review your site for mentions of your old domain name. Include images in your content audit. Learn more about content audits on the USDA's digital strategy playbook.

  7. Step 6: Plan to redirect traffic from your old domain to your new one

    Use HTTP redirects from your old domain to your new domain so that links to your old site will be directed to your new online home. Use HTTP 302s for testing, and HTTP 301s to signal a permanent move to search engines.

    If you're using your old domain for web redirects, plan to keep the TLS (Transport Layer Security) certificate current for your older domain.
